I'm reading this paper due to a sentence in the abstract:

> Suppes’ theory neglected the fact that attributes, not objects, are the focus of measurement and when that oversight is corrected, it follows that empirical systems sustaining measurement already instantiate positive real numbers

Categorical Measurement Systems theory must have something interesting to say about representational measurement theory to be worth a damn. 

One approach would be to say that measurement is about signification, not representation, and work from a semiotic theory. Deely argued that these concepts were distinct and that representation was a "degenerate" form of signifying. Mari seems to have a similar critique in that representation is not sufficient for measurement. Mari focuses on the lack of relevance of the theory to actual measurement practice and develops a theory to address these problems. Mari also develops the distinction between measurement and evaluation, and it could be that the concept of representation captures the latter but not the former. 

Another approach to RMT would be to accept the rough equivalence of signifying and representing and argue that representational measurement theory is a flawed instantiation of measurement concepts. Since "representation" is already a well developed concept within category theory, this would imply that a functorial notion of representation extends important . Representation theory focuses on groups and vector spaces, while category theory extends this notion to monoids and modules.

Yet another would be to accept representational measurement theory and extend it's domain by generalizing it's core concepts. 

From the abstract, this paper argues that RMT does not have an appropriate notion of "property", and therefore begs the question. Since Mari develops much of their work on developing measurement theory from the "object-property" perspective, this paper may have something interesting to contribute.

Michell argues that a naturalistic, realistic philosophy of number can expose a "fallacy inherent to RMT".

Classical (Aristotelian?) measurement theory (at the turn of the 19th century) was based on three concepts: quantity, magnitude and ratio. Quantity meant quantitative attribute. Magnitude was a "specific degree" of a quantitative attribute, but not necessarily a number. Ratios were a relation between magnitudes of the same kind. 

Hölder demonstrated that the system of all ratios of magnitudes were isomorphic to the positive real numbers. This eventually became a paradigm, where

> real numbers are instantiated in nature in ratios of magnitudes and measurement is the estimation of such ratios.

Bertand Russell sought to distinguish the concept of number from it's physical instantiation in quantity, stating in 1903:

> Measurement of magnitudes is, in its most general sense, any method by which a unique and reciprocal correspondence is established between all or some of the magnitudes of a kind and all or some of the numbers, integral, rational, or real, as the case may be.

This notion of isomorphism is very strong and would imply that a functorial understanding of representation would be too weak to count as measurement.

Aside: According to Lawvere, taking categories seriously implies attributing metaphysical importance to concepts such as universal properties and adjoint functor. Lawvere argues that subjective logic lies within a universe of discourse, that thought must move between different universes of discourse, and that the tools of subjective logic when applied to transformations between such universes can result in nonsense. 

Stevens expanded the notion of measurement to include ordinal, nominal, interval and ratio scales. Only the last was included in classical measurement theory. 

Edwin B. Newman, Stevens' colleague at Harvard, stated:

> We are asserting thereby that there exists a significant isomorphism between the two, the mathematics on the one hand and empirical science on the other. Yet if they are isomorphic they are both instances of some common structure. This should tell us something. (Newman, 1974, p. 143)

Suppes developed Stevens' ideas using Tarski's model theory. So this points to whether the categorical notion of model would produce the same sort of measurement theory as would Tarski's model theory.

Aside: David Corfield has an old nlab discussion of (Category theory and model theory)[https://golem.ph.utexas.edu/category/2008/07/category_theory_and_model_theo.html]. He points to the existence of the book *Accessible Categories: The Foundations of Categorical Model Theory* [@makkai:89:accessible]. They consider categorical model theory as narrower than categorical logic as 

> the study of set-valued models of possibly infinitary first order theories by means of the conceptual tools of category theory.

This appears to be a very deep domain. It's not possible from my current understanding to see if there is an implication for a possible direction for developing a category-theoretic version of representational measurement theory. Again, the study of measurement systems theory has deep connections to a lot of interesting ideas in philosophy, science and math.
